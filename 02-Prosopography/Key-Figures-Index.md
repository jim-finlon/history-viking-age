## How to Use This Index

Each figure is listed with:
- **Name** (Old Norse form on first mention)
- **Dates** (approximate where uncertain; marked with ~)
- **Role/Region**
- **Source Status** (see Confidence Levels in [[CLAUDE]])
- **Research Status** ([E] Enumerated, [S] Stubbed, [X] Expanded, [V] Verified)

**CRITICAL NOTE:** Many famous "Viking" figures are known primarily or exclusively from saga literature written 200-400 years after their lifetimes. Entries tagged [Saga Tradition] or [Legendary] reflect this. See [[CLAUDE]] for full source methodology.

**Notes on Ragnar Lothbrok:** The "sons of Ragnar" are more historically grounded than Ragnar himself. The ASC mentions "sons of Ragnar" as leaders of the Great Heathen Army, but whether there was a single father figure named Ragnar is unverifiable. The saga of Ragnar Lothbrok is a legendary tale, not a historical biography. Multiple historical figures may have been conflated into the Ragnar legend. Fiction writers should be aware that virtually everything popularly "known" about Ragnar comes from saga tradition and modern TV adaptation, not from contemporary sources.

| 49 | Gormlaith | Gormlaith (Irish name) | d. ~1030 | [Probable] -- Irish sources. Queen of Dublin, Leinster, and Munster through successive marriages. Present at the political crisis leading to the Battle of Clontarf. A pivotal figure in Irish-Norse politics. | [E] |

**Notes on Women:** The evidence for women in the Viking Age is a patchwork of saga tradition, runic inscriptions, archaeological finds, and occasional mentions in external sources. Judith Jesch's *Women in the Viking Age* remains the foundational study. The archaeological evidence (Oseberg, Birka Bj 581, wealthy female graves throughout Scandinavia) confirms that some women held high status. Legal rights documented in later Icelandic law (property ownership, divorce initiation) may reflect earlier customs but this extrapolation is [Debated].
